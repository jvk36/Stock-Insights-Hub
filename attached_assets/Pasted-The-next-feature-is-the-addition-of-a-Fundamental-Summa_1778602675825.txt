The next feature is the addition of a Fundamental Summary tab. It should be grouped into the following sections and corresponding metrics: a) Profitability Pillar (Efficiency & Moat): 1) Return on Equity (ROE) – measures how effectively management is using shareholder capital – color-coded with a percentage figure, >15% is good, 2) Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) – shows profit generated for every dollar of capital (debt+equity) put into the business – color-coded with a percentage figure, 10%+ higher compared to WACC is excellent, 2% to 5% higher is good, etc., 3) Pricing Power – flat or expanding gross margins over the last five years is good, 4) Efficiency Factor – Measure Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) using the standard formula Days Sales Outstanding PLUS Days Inventory Outstanding MINUS Days Payable Outstanding – Negative CCC is excellent as it means they get paid by customers before they have to pay suppliers, b) Valuation Pillar: 1) EV/EBITDA - Better than a standard P/E ratio because it accounts for debt levels and is harder to "fudge" with accounting tricks, 2) Free Cash Flow Yield: Ratio of FCF to Market-Cap - This tells you how much actual cash the company generates relative to its stock price. A 5% yield is often seen as a healthy baseline, 3) Price-to-Tangible Book-Value: For asset-heavy industries, this ensures you aren't paying too much for "goodwill" or brand names that might evaporate, c) Solvency & Health Pillar: 1) Net Debt to EBITDA – How many years of profit would it take to pay off all debt? <3.0x is generally healthy and >5.0x is a red flag (color-coded), 2) Interest Coverage Ratio – Can they pay their lenders? - >3.0x is healthy. If it drops below 1.5, the dividend (if any) is at high risk – color-coded, 3) Current to Quick Ratio: Measures short-term liquidity. >1.0 is healthy meaning they have more cash & receivables than bills due this year, d) Qualitative Pillar: 1) Insider Ownership – Are the captains of the ship also owners? Higher is better, 2) R&D as % of Revenue – monitors if the company is reinvesting for the future or just “harvesting” old products, 3) Share Count Trend - Is the company diluting you (issuing shares) or rewarding you (buybacks)? A declining share count increases your "slice of the pie." - Indicate percentage increase/decrease of shares outstanding over the last five years. Could you implement this tab?
